Pricing Your Coaching Services: A Complete Guide

January 5, 2026 • 10 min read

Pricing is where many coaches get stuck. Charge too little, and you'll burn out serving too many clients for too little return. Charge too much before you've built credibility, and you won't get clients at all.

Let's cut through the confusion with a practical approach to pricing your coaching services.

Understanding the Market

Coaching rates vary enormously based on niche, experience, and target market. Here's a rough landscape for 2026:

  • Entry-level life coaching: $75-150/session
  • Established life/wellness coaching: $150-300/session
  • Career coaching: $150-350/session
  • Business coaching: $200-500/session
  • Executive coaching: $300-1,000+/session
  • Premium/celebrity coaching: $1,000-5,000+/session

These are rough guidelines. Your specific positioning within a niche matters more than the niche itself.

The Value-Based Pricing Mindset

Stop thinking about what your time is worth. Start thinking about what the transformation is worth to your client.

Consider:

  • A career coach who helps someone land a job paying $30k more per year
  • An executive coach who helps a leader avoid a costly mistake
  • A health coach who helps someone avoid chronic disease
  • A relationship coach who saves a marriage

The ROI on effective coaching often exceeds the investment by 10x or more. Price accordingly.

Packages vs. Sessions

Most successful coaches have moved away from selling individual sessions. Here's why packages work better:

  • Better client outcomes: Transformation takes time. Single sessions rarely create lasting change.
  • Predictable revenue: You know what's coming in each month.
  • Client commitment: When clients invest in a package, they're more likely to do the work.
  • Reduced admin: Less time selling, more time coaching.

Common package structures:

  • 3-month package: 6-12 sessions, good for specific goals
  • 6-month package: 12-24 sessions, deeper transformation
  • 12-month package: Ongoing support, often includes additional access

Structuring Your Offerings

Consider a tiered approach:

Tier 1: Entry Point

A lower-investment option for clients who aren't ready for full commitment. This might be:

  • A single strategy session
  • A mini-package (3-4 sessions)
  • A group program

Tier 2: Core Offering

Your main package—where most clients land. This is typically a 3-6 month engagement with regular sessions.

Tier 3: Premium

High-touch, intensive support for clients who want maximum access. Might include:

  • More frequent sessions
  • Between-session access (Voxer, Slack, email)
  • Additional resources or assessments
  • In-person components

How to Raise Your Prices

If you've been undercharging (most coaches have), here's how to course-correct:

  1. Raise prices for new clients first. Honor existing agreements.
  2. Add value when you increase price. New resources, longer sessions, additional support.
  3. Increase incrementally. 10-20% raises feel more natural than doubling overnight.
  4. Communicate value, not justification. You don't need to explain or apologize.
  5. Be prepared for some "no's." That's actually a good sign—you were too cheap before.

Payment Options

Make it easy for clients to say yes:

  • Pay in full discount: Offer 5-10% off for upfront payment. Improves your cash flow.
  • Payment plans: Split packages into monthly payments. More accessible for clients.
  • Credit card processing: Accept cards even if fees eat into your margin. Convenience matters.

Common Pricing Mistakes

  • Underpricing to fill your practice: You'll attract price-sensitive clients and burn out.
  • Copying competitors: Their situation isn't yours. Price based on your value and costs.
  • Not accounting for expenses: Remember taxes, insurance, software, marketing, etc.
  • Discounting too easily: Every discount trains clients that your prices are negotiable.
  • Avoiding the money conversation: Discomfort around pricing undermines your authority.

The Formula

Here's a simple way to sanity-check your pricing:

  1. Determine your annual income goal
  2. Decide how many clients you can serve well (most coaches max out at 15-20 at a time)
  3. Calculate how many client slots you'll fill per year (accounting for turnover)
  4. Divide your income goal by client slots = minimum average client value

If the math doesn't work, you either need to raise prices or add leverage (group programs, courses, etc.).

Final Thought

Pricing is a skill you develop over time. You'll probably underprice at first. You'll probably feel uncomfortable raising rates. That's normal.

What matters is that you keep adjusting until your pricing reflects your value and supports a sustainable practice. The clients who are right for you will pay what you're worth. The ones who won't? They weren't your clients anyway.
